Anonymous Authors

Abstract

Automatic lip reading is considered to be one of the most important assistive technologies for the deaf community, but generic models often struggle to lip read due to the unique articulatory patterns of deaf speakers. This paper presents a personalized approach to address this challenge. We collect data from multiple deaf speakers and propose a method to adapt state-of-the-art lip reading models for individual users. Our approach employs layer-specific fine-tuning to identify the most effective parameters for speaker-specific learning, resulting in a parameter-efficient adaptation process. Experimental results show significant improvements in word error rates compared to generic models.

Dataset Collection and Annotation

Annotation Website




During annotation, we are given a short video clip of the speaker, we start by carefully viewing the video and analyse the mouth movements. If we find errors in the caption provided by the youtuber, we modify the caption according to what the person has actually spoken, and then save the changes, subsequently moving to the next clip. We repeat this for all the clips of the speaker, which are around 800 on average per speaker.


Results

Fine-Tuning Experiments



We identify the important parts of the Auto-AVSR architecture, which are crucial for personalization. We follow a systematic approach to choose which layers to finetune, and finally go down till the mid-conv layers of the conformer, being the most parameter efficient. Just 9% of the parameters contributes to 50% of the personalization. Also, fine-tuning only the encoder leads to equivalent performance to full model fine-tuning.


Amount of Data Variation



We investigate the effect of the amount of data on the level of personalization. For this, we fine-tuned the model with varying amounts of data. We observe that full fine-tuning the model on just ten minutes and twenty minutes of the data gives 38.3% and 65.7% of improvement as compared to the model trained on 40 minutes of the data.
